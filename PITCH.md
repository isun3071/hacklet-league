nobody has. because until recently, building software took too long to make watchable.
okay so. introducing the HackLet League. hacklets, like little hackathons, except where a normal hackathon takes 24 hours, a hacklet takes 24 minutes. (yeah we kind of messed up the units. we picked minutes instead of hours. but turns out AI compresses what an engineer can do that much. it works out.)
here's what you're watching. eight CS majors sit down at workstations on a stage. they get a sanctioned AI to work with. they get 24 minutes to build a real functional web app. there's no theme. they choose what to build. you watch them build it on stream — all eight workstations broadcast simultaneously, picture-in-picture, with director cuts to whoever's about to do something interesting. you read their chat with the AI in real time, which is wild because you've never seen anyone's thinking in real time before, not in any other competitive format. their token budget shows on the overlay. their slop score updates live, ticking down as they patch what the probes find. you see who's gambling on minimal defensive testing and who's hammering on every probe.
at 24 minutes everything freezes. code locks. and then the real drama starts.
an automated adversarial test catalog runs against each player's submission. SQL injection. cross-site scripting. auth bypass. file upload exploits. crash resistance. error hygiene. the universal arsenal of attacks that real production code has to survive. every probe that breaks an app adds slop. a clean app stays at zero, and zero is perfect. lower is better. an app that doesn't even deploy is a DNF, the worst outcome of all, never a clean zero. the catalog is deterministic, the same probes against every submission, which is why scores from one round are comparable to scores from another. this is the part that makes wins mean something.
then they pitch what they built. 60 seconds of pitch. 120 seconds of cross-examination from four judges, including a tester judge who actually reads their code, a UX designer who clicked through their app, and a stakeholder judge whose entire job is making sure the player can explain technical work to someone who isn't deep in it. (yeah, we built that as a scored dimension. cross-functional communication is career-determining at senior engineering levels. now it's also a category you can win.)
then awards. categorical awards first — Most Resilient (lowest slop score), Best Communicator (pitch and cross-ex combined), People's Hacklet (audience vote). then Best Overall, the composite champion. then it cuts to the next round. multi-day tournaments build to championship finals on day three, single-panel finals, eight finalists, one champion.
it's MasterChef for engineering. it's FMWC for vibecoding. it's what FIDE is for chess, except for what CS majors actually do now in 2026.
vibecoding became Collins Dictionary's 2025 Word of the Year. there's a book about it by Gene Kim and Steve Yegge. Karpathy already proposed retiring the term and replacing it with "agentic engineering." the whole industry is figuring out what AI-assisted engineering means as a credentialed skill. HackLet is where that figuring-out happens as a sport.
come watch.

Pitch 2: Tier A Participant — CS Majors Considering Elite Competition

okay so you've been using AI to code for two years now and you've probably noticed a few things.
first: school is unclear about whether you can use it. your internships are unclear. your friends are unclear. there's this ambient guilt about how much AI you're using even though everyone is using it.
second: leetcode is increasingly meaningless. you can solve any leetcode problem with the AI in your pocket. that whole signal has collapsed for senior hiring.
third: CTFs are eroding. the AI security community has openly acknowledged that top frontier models can now one-shot CTF challenges that used to require hours or days of skilled human effort. the scoreboard at most CTFs no longer measures human skill but rather the ability to orchestrate AI agents and spend on compute tokens. the credentialing pipeline that produced top security talent for two decades is being broken by the tool. TWiT.tv
fourth: hackathons are increasingly weird. half the teams are vibecoding, half are trying to preserve the "no AI" purity of an earlier era, and nobody knows what the credential means anymore.
HackLet says all of that openly. and then says: use the AI. that's the whole point. it's not the thing you're not supposed to use, it's the thing you're being scored on how well you direct.
here's the format at Tier A. eight players. controlled workstations. league firewall to a sanctioned AI substrate — DeepSeek V4 Flash for Season 1, accessed through both a chat-window interface and an in-IDE agent extension with shared token budget so you can't just tool-stack your way to advantage. you get 100,000 tokens for the round, hard cap, server enforced. you get a curated package mirror with all the major web frameworks (Flask, FastAPI, Express, Fastify, Spring Boot, ASP.NET, Laravel — you pick). you get a local IDE. you get 24 minutes.
build a web app. anything you want. no required theme.
at 24 minutes everything freezes. your code auto-captures to league infrastructure via SCP from your workstation. we deploy it in an ephemeral container. we run the full adversarial catalog — public pool (you can study it before competition) plus hidden pool (you can't). SQL injection. XSS. CSRF. auth bypass. file upload. attack surface enumeration through endpoint discovery probes. QA edge cases. performance under load. reliability under failure conditions. deployment hygiene. every probe that breaks an app adds slop. a clean app stays at zero. lower is better.
then live pitch. 60 seconds of pitch. 120 seconds of cross-examination from four judges in turn — the tester judge reads your code, the UX designer clicked your app, the general engineering judge weighs your architecture, the stakeholder judge wants to know if you can explain why any of this matters to someone who doesn't speak your stack. each judge gets one substantive question. verbose answers cost remaining slots.
what this format rewards: knowing what to defend before being told. directing AI toward robust work, not just working work. catching hallucinated dependencies and fabricated API calls before they ship. reading code critically under time pressure. explaining your decisions like an engineer who's about to be a senior engineer.
what it doesn't reward: typing speed. framework memorization. refusing to use AI. slop with confidence. jargon shields. resource-maxxing through the substrate.
Tier A operates with structural anti-cheating — RMM-controlled workstations, network firewall with allowlist to league infrastructure only, ephemeral per-round Unix accounts, audit-logged AI proxy, broadcast streaming with player consent. wins contribute to global league rankings. credentials carry employer-interpretable signal of capability rather than substrate access. multi-day regional and championship tournaments use snake-draft pod composition, top-4-per-pod advancement, single-panel finals with the same eight finalists evaluated against each other. champions accumulate progressively richer credentials: variant champion → format overall → cross-format championship → sustained multi-year champion.
the easiest way to think about it: every senior code review you'll ever get is going to ask "why did you do it this way" and "what happens if a user does X." HackLet Tier A is that, in 24 minutes, with an automated adversary, in front of cameras, with the receipts going on your resume.
if you can hold up under that you can hold up in any real engineering job. and the credential says so explicitly.

Pitch 3: Tier C Participant — BU Pilot

okay so. you're at BU. you're CS. you've used AI for everything for the last two years. you've watched leetcode credentials collapse and hackathons get weird. you've maybe seen the HackLet League content floating around — the Tier A stuff with controlled workstations and broadcast cameras and global rankings.
that's the destination. this is the path.
we're piloting HackLet at BU through UPE this fall. every other Wednesday. 60 minutes total, fits inside a normal club meeting slot.
format: 5 minutes opening, 24 minutes build, 18 minutes pitch writing while we fuzz your submission, 5 minutes LLM-judging your pitch, 8 minutes awards and closing.
specifically, this is HackLet's Minimum Viable Round. the smallest version of the format that's still substantively HackLet. it's deliberately accessible. bring your own laptop. bring your own AI — Claude, ChatGPT, Cursor, Cline, whatever fits your workflow. we don't host the AI at this tier and we don't enforce token budgets. web search is fine. anything goes. (the Tier A version of HackLet locks all that down with controlled substrate and enforced budgets. that's for credentialing-grade events with global rankings. this is club-meeting-accessible MVR, not credentialing-grade — both honest, both real, just different scope.)
you'll build a web app from scratch in 24 minutes. there's a prompt at the start, no required theme beyond it. use whatever framework you like — Flask, FastAPI, Express, Next.js, whatever. when the timer hits 24, you upload your code through the league portal (3-minute grace period for upload latency). server-side we deploy your code in an ephemeral container and run the adversarial fuzz catalog against it. SQL injection. XSS. CSRF. auth bypass. file upload exploits. attack surface enumeration. QA edge cases. crash resistance. error hygiene. the catalog is the same one Tier A uses — chapter-local credential, same adversary.
while we're fuzzing your code, you write PITCH.md — a structured markdown document where you articulate what you built, your key defensive choices, what you prioritized, what you didn't get to, what fuzz response you're expecting, and how you directed AI during build. it's a template, sections provided, you fill it in. you can use AI to help you write it. that's consistent with the format — the substrate's AI-augmented across all phases, not just build.
then LLM-judging. we send your PITCH.md plus README plus fuzz results to a multi-model judge panel (Claude, GPT, Gemini in parallel) running through OpenRouter Fusion. three sysprompts run in parallel: technical evaluation, nontechnical stakeholder evaluation, comparative cross-submission evaluation. you get scored on Communication that's evaluated across technical depth and cross-functional translation. the dry-start architecture and panel diversity mean the LLMs aren't recognizing each other's writing in ways that bias the eval.
categorical awards: Most Resilient (lowest slop score), Best Communicator (LLM-evaluated articulation), Best Overall (composite champion). People's Hacklet if there's audience present.
what's honest about Tier C credentials: chapter-local only. doesn't contribute to any global ranking. doesn't claim substrate equality (you brought your own AI, your friend brought a different one, the credential doesn't pretend those are equivalent). credentials Tier C as: demonstrated AI-augmented engineering capability against deterministic adversarial testing within the BU chapter, with demonstrated written articulation under time compression. real engagement signal, peer-network involvement, technical community membership. not the global credential — Tier A is for that. but real signal at chapter scope.
what's substantively interesting about playing at the MVR floor: the format runs every other Wednesday. weekly cadence builds skill faster than monthly events. you develop the verification reflex, the defensive thinking, the AI direction discipline, the technical communication — all the durable skills that the eventual Tier A version of HackLet will credential at credentialing-grade. this is the path that gets there.
